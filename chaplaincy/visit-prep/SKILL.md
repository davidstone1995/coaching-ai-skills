# Visit Prep (Team Care)

**Requires:** coaching-foundation

---

## What This Skill Does

A chaplaincy visit is not a coaching session. There is no intake form, no Coaching Sandwich, no defined outcome. The goal is to show up, be present, and make contact with the people in the room. That is the whole job.

This skill helps David prepare for that. It produces a brief note -- not a script, not an agenda -- that helps him walk in ready to be human with the people he is serving.

---

## What You Need to Provide

**Required:**
- Visit context: MSU athletics, corporate client, or other Team Care context
- Date and location if relevant

**Helpful:**
- Names of people he is likely to see (especially anyone who has been on his radar)
- Anything he knows is happening in the group (big game, hard week, someone going through something)
- Notes from a prior visit that are worth carrying in
- Specific people to make sure he connects with

If David walks in with nothing prepared, that is fine too -- this skill can generate a general prep note from just the context.

---

## Step 1: Identify the Visit Context

### MSU Athletics (Team Care)

The primary chaplaincy relationship. Missouri State University football and athletic department. In the room with players, coaches, staff. The relationships have been building since 2021. David knows many of these people personally.

**What matters here:**
- Athletes are under performance pressure. They do not want a chaplain who feels like another obligation.
- Presence is the ministry. Sitting in a room, laughing at something, asking a question that shows you remembered -- that is what builds trust.
- Faith conversations open through relationship. They are not rushed. They are not manufactured.
- The locker room culture: brief, direct, human. No religious language that would feel out of place there.

### Corporate / Organizational Client

A retained chaplaincy arrangement with a company or organization. Team Care Starter, Growth, or Full package. Regular rounds, as-needed conversations, crisis response.

**What matters here:**
- Not every person is open to faith. Presence does not require faith language.
- The relationship with leadership matters -- they are the ones who brought Team Care in. Their trust is what keeps the door open.
- Employees who are going through hard things often need someone who is not their manager or HR. That is the role.

## Step 2: Generate the Visit Prep Note

Brief. One page or less. David reads this on the drive over or in the parking lot before he walks in.

---

**VISIT PREP NOTE**
[Context: MSU Athletics / Corporate Client Name] | [Date] | Team Care

---

**Who is likely in the room**

[Names or roles David expects to see. If specific people are flagged, note them here -- not a roster, just the ones who matter for this visit. If nothing is known, note that and move on.]

**What is happening right now**

[Anything David knows about the current season, circumstance, or atmosphere. A game coming up. A loss. A hard week. A transition in the organization. If nothing is known, leave this brief: "Nothing specific flagged. Arrive open."]

**People to make sure he reaches**

[Specific names or roles David wants to make sure he connects with on this visit -- not because something is wrong, but because the relationship is worth tending. 2-3 names maximum. If none are flagged, this section reads: "No one flagged. Let the room guide it."]

**Something to carry in**

[One thing David can reference in conversation that shows he was paying attention last time. A name someone mentioned. Something that was happening. A question that was left open. This is the thing that makes a visit feel personal rather than routine. If no prior context is available: "First visit -- arrive curious. Ask names. Remember them."]

**How to open**

[Not a script. One or two natural openers calibrated to the context. Examples:
- "Hey, how'd the week treat you?"
- "I heard [thing] happened -- how are you all doing with that?"
- "I've been thinking about you guys. What's the vibe this week?"

Keep it human. Keep it brief. Let them lead from there.]

**What presence looks like today**

[One short paragraph. What does a good visit look like for this specific context on this specific day? Not a list of tasks. A picture. Example: "Walk the room, don't sit in one spot. If someone is alone, go to them first. Stay until a natural ending point, not a scheduled one. Leave people feeling like they were seen."]

**One thing to carry out**

[After the visit, what should David remember to note? A name, a situation, someone who needs follow-up. This seeds the care-log entry after the visit.]

---

## Output Quality Check

Before delivering the prep note:

- Note is one page or less
- No curriculum, no session arc, no agenda
- Tone is human and present, not professional and structured
- "People to reach" list has no more than 3 names
- "Something to carry in" is specific and real, not generic
- Nothing sounds like a pastoral visit template from a denominational handbook
- No em dashes
- No banned phrases from coaching-foundation
- Entity is Team Care (not Cornerstone)
