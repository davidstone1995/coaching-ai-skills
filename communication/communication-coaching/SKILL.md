# Communication Coaching

**Requires:** coaching-foundation

---

## What This Skill Does

Communication coaching is one of the most practical things David does. The client has a presentation to give, a hard conversation to navigate, a team they can't seem to reach, or a voice that is not landing the way they intend. David has been communicating publicly three to four times a week for thirty years. That experience is the credential. This skill helps him bring it to a client efficiently.

This skill produces a session prep brief for communication coaching work, a feedback framework for reviewing a client's recent communication, and practical exercises calibrated to the client's specific development area.

---

## What You Need to Provide

**Required:**
- Client name and type
- What the client is working on (choose one or describe):
  - Clarity: their message is muddled; people leave unsure what to do
  - Structure: their communication lacks a clear arc; it wanders
  - Presence: they are technically competent but not connecting; flat delivery
  - Confidence: they know what they want to say but something gets in the way when they say it
  - Audience awareness: they are communicating to themselves, not to the person in front of them
  - Specific situation: a presentation, a difficult conversation, a media appearance, a team address

**Helpful:**
- Any prior work David has done with this client in this area
- Any specific piece of communication they want to prepare or debrief (a talk, a conversation, a presentation)
- What feedback the client has already received (so David does not simply repeat it)
- The highest-stakes upcoming communication moment this client has

## Step 1: Identify the Development Area

### Clarity

The client knows what they think but cannot organize it into a message others can follow. Their communication contains the right content, poorly arranged.

**Root issue:** Usually one of three things -- they are trying to say too much at once, they do not yet know what the one main point is, or they are working something out in real time rather than having worked it out first.

**Coaching focus:** Find the one main point. Everything else serves it or gets cut.

### Structure

The communication has content but no arc. It starts somewhere and ends somewhere else. The listener cannot follow the sequence or cannot tell when they are at the important part.

**Root issue:** The communicator is following their own thinking process rather than designing an experience for the listener.

**Coaching focus:** The audience is on a journey. The communicator's job is to know where they are taking them before they start. Me/We/God/You/We (for sermon/ministry contexts) or a simpler problem/solution/invitation arc for business contexts.

### Presence

The client is technically competent but not connecting. Their delivery is flat, mechanical, over-rehearsed, or anxious. The content is fine but nothing lands.

**Root issue:** Usually one of two things: they are performing communication rather than having a conversation, or they are so focused on what comes next that they are not fully in the moment they are in.

**Coaching focus:** Communication is conversation at scale. The goal is not performance -- it is contact. One person, fully present, talking to one other person at a time even in a room of five hundred.

### Confidence

The client knows what to say but something breaks down in the delivery. Hedging language ("I think maybe..."), physical self-consciousness, rushing, avoiding eye contact, apologizing before they have said anything wrong.

**Root issue:** Usually a belief about themselves or the audience ("they know more than I do," "I'm going to say something wrong," "they don't want to hear this from me").

**Coaching focus:** Confidence is not something you feel first and then communicate -- it is something you practice until the feeling follows the behavior. Start with the behavior.

### Audience Awareness

The client communicates as though the audience is already where the communicator is -- already informed, already convinced, already caring. They skip the steps that get the listener there.

**Root issue:** The communicator is inside the content. They cannot see it from the outside.

**Coaching focus:** Always ask: what does my audience know, believe, and feel right now before I start speaking? Everything in the communication is designed to move them from where they are to where I need them to be.

## Step 2: Generate the Communication Coaching Package

Produce three components.

---

### COMPONENT 1: Session Prep Brief

**[Client Name] | Communication Coaching | Session [#] | [Date]**

**Development area:** [Clarity / Structure / Presence / Confidence / Audience awareness / Specific situation]

**What they are working toward:** [One sentence. Not the general skill -- the specific thing this client needs to be able to do better. Example: "Present a business proposal to an unfamiliar audience without hedging every other sentence."]

**What to open with:** [The first question or prompt for the session. Something that puts them in the work right away. Example: "Walk me through the last time you gave a presentation. Take me to a specific moment where something felt off." Or: "Tell me what you're going to say on Thursday. Not the slides -- tell me." ]

**What to listen for:** [Patterns or behaviors specific to this client. What usually shows up? What does their communication do when it is not working? 2-3 specific things to notice.]

**Coaching questions to hold:**
[3-5 questions calibrated to this client's development area. Not a script -- questions held lightly.]

Clarity examples:
- "If someone left this conversation knowing only one thing, what do you want that to be?"
- "What are you trying to say that you haven't said yet?"
- "What would you cut if you had half the time?"

Structure examples:
- "Walk me through the experience from the listener's side. What do they know at the start? What do they need to know to get to the end?"
- "Where is the moment in this communication where everything else is pointing?"
- "What problem are you solving for them? Have you said that out loud yet?"

Presence examples:
- "What happens in your body right before you start speaking?"
- "What would it look like if you were having this conversation with one person you trusted completely?"
- "What are you thinking about while you are speaking?"

Confidence examples:
- "What are you afraid they are going to think?"
- "What would you say if you knew the outcome didn't matter?"
- "What do you know about this topic that they don't? Start there."

Audience awareness examples:
- "Before you open your mouth -- what does this person believe right now about this topic?"
- "At what point in this communication are you expecting them to care? Have you earned that yet?"
- "What's the version of this that would have worked on you five years ago?"

**Practice moment:** [If applicable, a specific exercise to do in the session. See Component 3.]

---

### COMPONENT 2: Feedback Framework

Use this when reviewing a specific piece of communication the client has given or is preparing. Works for a talk, a presentation, a difficult conversation they are rehearsing, or a recorded delivery.

**Three-layer feedback structure:**

**Layer 1 -- What is working**
[What to name specifically. Do not rush past this. Clients with confidence issues especially need to hear the real strengths named first -- not as a preamble to criticism but as genuine recognition. Be specific: not "you were clear" but "your opening sentence named exactly what you were going to do and you delivered it."]

**Layer 2 -- The one thing to change**
[One. Not three. Not "a few things." The single most important development point. If there are multiple issues, pick the one that, if fixed, would solve the others. Be specific and behavioral: not "be more confident" but "stop apologizing before you start. The next time you open your mouth, your first sentence is a statement, not an apology."]

**Layer 3 -- The practice prescription**
[A specific, concrete exercise the client can do before the next session. See Component 3 for exercises by development area.]

---

### COMPONENT 3: Practical Exercises (by development area)

Assign one exercise per session. One at a time.

**Clarity exercises:**
- *The one-sentence version:* "Write your entire message in one sentence. Not a paragraph. One sentence. Then build everything else around that."
- *The cut drill:* "Take what you have. Cut it by half. Then cut it by half again. What survived is what matters."
- *The stranger test:* "Explain this to someone who knows nothing about it. What do they ask? Those are the gaps."

**Structure exercises:**
- *The audience journey map:* "Write down three things. (1) What does my audience believe/know/feel at the start? (2) What do I need them to believe/know/feel at the end? (3) What is the sequence of steps that gets them there?"
- *The headline test:* "Write the headline. What is the single sentence someone would use to describe this communication to someone who wasn't there?"
- *Reverse outline:* "Give the talk. Then write the outline from memory. Compare it to what you intended. Where do they diverge?"

**Presence exercises:**
- *The one person:* "Pick one person in the room. Have the whole conversation with them. Then move to the next person. Never speak to a crowd -- always speak to a person."
- *The pause practice:* "After every major point, pause. Count to three silently. Do not fill the silence. Let it land."
- *Slow down by 20%:* "Whatever pace feels natural to you, it is too fast. Slow down by 20%. The pause feels longer to you than to the audience."

**Confidence exercises:**
- *The preparation ritual:* "Know your opening cold. Not roughly -- cold. The first 60 seconds, word for word, body and voice fully committed. Confidence builds from a stable start."
- *Stand differently:* "Plant your feet. Do not shift. Do not back up. Take up the space you are in. Your body tells your audience before you open your mouth whether you believe you belong there."
- *Reframe the audience:* "They are not evaluating you. They are hoping you are going to give them something useful. They want you to win. Act accordingly."

**Audience awareness exercises:**
- *The belief inventory:* "Before you prepare anything, write down what your audience believes about this topic right now. Be honest. Build your communication to meet them where they are and move them from there."
- *The question they are already asking:* "What is the question your audience has that you are answering? If you do not know what question they are asking, you are answering the wrong question."

## Output Quality Check

Before delivering the package:

- Session prep brief has a specific opening move, not a generic coaching opener
- Coaching questions are calibrated to the development area and this specific client
- Feedback framework gives one thing to change -- not a list
- Exercise assigned is specific and doable before the next session
- No em dashes
- No banned phrases from coaching-foundation
- Every example models the communication quality being coached (short, clear, direct)
